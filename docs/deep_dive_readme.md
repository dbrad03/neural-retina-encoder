# Deep Dive: Neuro-Biological Choices in the Science Eye FPGA

## 1. The Challenge of Visual Prosthetics
Current visual prosthetics often rely on direct, uniform electrical stimulation of the optic nerve or visual cortex. However, simply sending an array of voltages (like pixels on a screen) directly into the nervous system fails to create natural vision. The human optic nerve expects signals encoded as complex, asynchronous "spikes" generated by the retina. By failing to provide these spike trains, traditional prosthetics result in visual noise and poor spatial resolution.

The **Science Eye FPGA** bridges this gap by acting as a biological emulator. Instead of raw pixels, it translates light intensity into biologically *inspired* spike patterns using the Izhikevich neuron model before the signal is passed onto the biological interface.

## 2. Retinal Ganglion Cells: Midget vs. Parasol
The human retina does not process light uniformly. The ganglion cells that transmit signals to the brain have distinctly different roles depending on their location in the eye. We model the two most prominent types:

### Midget Cells (The Fovea)
- **Role:** High-acuity vision, color, and fine detail.
- **Location:** Densely packed in the fovea (the center of vision).
- **Behavior:** These cells exhibit a *Regular Spiking (RS)* or steady response. When exposed to a continuous stimulus, they fire at a steady, consistent rate. They do not adapt or fade quickly, allowing us to stare at an object and maintain a clear, steady image of it.
- **Izhikevich Parameters:** `a = 0.02, b = 0.2, c = -65.0, d = 8.0`

### Parasol Cells (The Periphery)
- **Role:** Motion detection, contrast, and low-light vision.
- **Location:** Sparse, but dominate the peripheral retina.
- **Behavior:** These cells exhibit a *Chattering (CH)* or bursty response. When a stimulus appears, they fire a rapid burst of spikes and then quickly adapt, going quiet even if the stimulus remains. This makes them highly sensitive to changes (like a fast-moving object entering your peripheral vision) but terrible for sustained focus.
- **Izhikevich Parameters:** `a = 0.1, b = 0.2, c = -65.0, d = 2.0`

## 3. The Foveated Array Architecture
To replicate this biological structure efficiently on an FPGA, we implemented a **Foveated Mapping** strategy across our 128x128 neuron array (16,384 total neurons). 

Instead of treating the array as a uniform grid, the FPGA's controller mathematically calculates the radius of each neuron from the center (64, 64) via diamond-foveation logic. 
- **Inner Radii (The Fovea):** Neurons within the central region are assigned the Midget cell parameters. This gives the "patient" clear, steady vision where they are focusing.
- **Outer Radii (The Periphery):** Neurons outside this central zone are assigned the Parasol cell parameters. 

### Why Foveated Mapping?
1. **Biological Plausibility:** It more closely matches how the human eye works than a uniform pixel grid, aiming for a more natural sensory encoding (a modeling goal, not a clinically validated outcome).
2. **Data Bandwidth Reduction:** Because Parasol cells quickly adapt and stop firing for static images, the periphery of the 128x128 array produces significantly fewer spikes. This drastically reduces the data bandwidth required between the FPGA and the neural interface, saving power and avoiding signal bottlenecking.
3. **Power Efficiency:** Less spiking means less switching activity on the interface, which is critical for an implanted or wearable prosthetic targeting a power budget of `< 100mW`.

## 4. Hardware Optimization via the Izhikevich Model
We selected the Izhikevich model because it provides the biological accuracy of the complex Hodgkin-Huxley model but with the computational simplicity of a simple Integrate-and-Fire model. 

In the RTL implementation (`hdl/izh_neuron_engine.sv`), the model is converted entirely to **Q8.10 Fixed-Point Arithmetic**. This allows us to avoid costly floating-point DSP units on the FPGA. By time-multiplexing a single high-speed neuron engine to calculate the state of all 16,384 neurons sequentially within a 1ms biological time window, the system achieves maximum parallelism with minimal silicon area.
